Networked Health

Incorporate network information into models

Network analysis from Electronic Medical Records


Contributions:

  • Complex medical signals and imaging. Uncover the clinically relevant patterns automatically from signals. [Published in: AAAI'15].

  • One-sided filter bank for feature engineering in Electronic Medical Records. Medical records are noisy, episodic and irregular and thus creating great challenges for applying analytical tools. We invented a signal processing style method for extracting comprehensive features for any analysis tasks. [Published in: KDD'13, BMC Informatics'14]

  • Patient profiling. Building a unified representation of a patient is extremely useful for individual-level analysis. The challenges are in encoding diverse sources of temporal events. [Published in: PAKDD'13, AAAI'15].

  • Suicide risk prediction. This problem is extremely difficult due to rarity of suicide events and weak predictive risk factors. [Published in: KDD'13, KAIS, BMC Psychiatry].

  • Cancer mortality prediction. There are many cancers and the question is should we build a single modelf or all cancers or separate models for individual cancers? [Published in: BMJ Open]

  • Stabilizing clinical prediction models. High-dimensional prediction models are unstable under feature selection and lasso-like shrinkages, and thus less useful because they cannot be reliably estimated from similar sources. [Published in: KAIS, IEEE-JBHI, IWPRHA14, PAKDD'15].
  • Heart attack readmission prediction. [Published in: Aus Health Review] We show using readily collected electronic medical records would compete well with carefully collected clinical data.
  • Preterm birth prediction. [Under submission] Preterm birth is hard to predict. We introduce data engineering and machine learning techniques to crack the problem. Predictive accuracy (in terms of sensitivity, positive predictive value, and AUC) is excellent, almost twice as much as prior arts.
  • Heart failure readmission prediction. [Published in:  IEEE-JBHI] Again, eletronic medical records are informative, structurally rich and can be exploited to improve model stability and discrimination.
  • Modelling interventions. [Under submission] We show interventions can be used as patient stratification into groups from which prediction models can be more accurately developed.
  • Social media for health. We show Web search activities are accurate indicators of many social, economic and health issues [Published in: WISE14, JECH15]. Classification of mood in Weblogs [Published in PAKDD10].
  • Healthcare visual analytics. [Published in: HISABD14] We demonstrate how visual analytics of entire patient history can help doctors make better decision.
  • Vector representation of medical objects. A vector/matrix representation allows simple algebraic manipulations and coding of relations, where objects (disease, intervention, patient, doctor) live in the same high-dimensional space. [Published in: JBI15]
  • Learning patient similarity. [Under submission] Patient similarity is a fundamental concept that underlies evidence-based medicine. However, computing similarity often involves heuristics that are hard to justify. We propose a method that learn to represent patients and compute similarity between them at the same time.
  • Exploiting redundancy in medical data. [Under submission] Redundancy is often thought to be problematic in machine learning as it causes uncertainty in model construction. However, we demonstrate that redundancy can be exploited to improve accuracy and reduce model uncertainty.
  • Combining evidences from the literature. [Under submission] Medical studies of the same topic are often numerous but they often focus on separate perspectives and risk factors. We introduce a simple method for practictioners to combine existing individual evidences to build patient-level prediction models.
  • Analysis of activities of daily living (ADL). Recognizing indoor activities using cameras, learning under missing labels [Published in NIPS08,  PRICAI08, CVPR06, ISSNIP05].
