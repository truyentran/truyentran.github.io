Research areas

The Centre for Pattern Recognition and Data Analytics (PRaDA), led by Professor Svetha Venkatesh, is a multidisciplinary research body, currently undertaking research in the following areas:

AI Fundamentals

Our main focus is on the learning aspects of intelligence. See this blogpost for our view on machine learning. Major sub-areas:
  • Deep learning in that we find efficient ways for end-to-end learning with minimal human supervision. It achieves the goals through multiple abstractions and iterative estimation. Our sub-areas include, but not limited to, architecture engineerning, relational models, unsupervised learning, memory and attention, and data efficiency. See this blogpost on why deep learning works.
  • Nonparametric Bayesian methods where we address the limits of current parametric models by letting the complexity of the models grow with data and automatically determine the model size that fits the data.
  • Bayesian optimization in that we learn to seek high-quality solutions through quanlified uncertainty.

AI Applications

We want to use AI to make the world better. Here are a few things we are passionate about:

  • Personalized medicine in which we aim to (a) understand about patient's health, need, preference and intention through patient's diverse sources of data (medical records, genomics, user-generated media, medical imaging and biosignals), plus aquisition of and reasoning about established medical knowledge; (b) have a meaningful dialog with patients; (b) recommend the personalized course of medical actions; (d) support doctors and hospital managers to improve their precision and efficiency. See this blogpost for some futuristic ideas.
  • Automated software engineering in what we want to read the code, translate between languages, automate the programming process, understand developer and support team management. 
  • Computer vision in which we want to understand videos, read children stories, monitor streets, detect baby unusual movements. 
  • Cybersecurity through anomaly detection in that we detect unusual network behaviors without supervision.
  • Recommender systems in that we deliver personalized content to the right user.

Selection criteria

There is no single selection criterion and we can only say that we look for candidates who have good potential to have a successful research career, not just a PhD degree. If you do not know what it takes for a research career, I recommend that you should spend time to read about it. Sometimes it will help you decide whether you need a PhD or not. Here is the list of things we are looking for

  • Thirst of knowledge and willing to pursue a research career. PhD is just a stepping stone, and a by-product of the learning process.

  • Research experience (e.g. has published papers).

  • Academic recognition: university prizes and awards. High quality honours at high school (such as members of national or international teams) may also count. 

  • Bachelor/Master in Computer Science/Informatics/Information Technology with excellent academic performance from a well-recognised university. For those with Australian degrees, the standard requirement is an Honours degree. 

  • Good math background (we will use a lot of math for data modelling, likely probability/statistics and linear algebra)

  • Good programming skills

  • Good English (generally IELTS score 6.5 (with no bands below 6) or better is required for entry, but you should be aware that this score is not generally enough for smooth communication. Good reading skills with critical thinking are vital because you will spend up to 50% of your time for reading. Technical writing is also important because it is often the end-product for assessment.)

What to send

The rule of thumb is that you should send whatever (electronic) documents you think that will support your future research career. Before you send these, I recommend that you spend time to study research areas and publications by PRaDA. Documents are written in English and may include some of the following

  • A comprehensive resume (or CV)

  • Academic transcripts

  • Theses

  • Published papers or technical reports (if written in languages other than English, titles and abstracts should be translated to English)

  • A statement of purpose briefly describes your research intention and interest, general approach to reach your PhD/career goals

  • Any supporting documents, like reference letters from your professors

You can send your documents directly to Dr Truyen Tran.
